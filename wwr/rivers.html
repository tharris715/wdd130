Our Rivers

Main Fork of the Salmon River, Idaho

Nestled deep in the Frank Church Wilderness of No Return winds the Salmon river. The Salmon was nicknamed the River of No Return by the residents because of those who came to visit and never left after falling in love with the beauty of the river.

Herds of elk and deer graze on the hillsides around the river. You may also see bear and bighorn sheep. The river corridor is lined with awe inspiring ponderosa pines which tower over the sandy beaches of the campsites and provide shelter from sun or rain when needed.

The rapids range from class I-IV. The Salmon is one of the longest undammed rivers in the United States, meaning the water is higher, faster, and colder in the early summer and lower, slower, and warmer in the late summer. There are good rapids all summer, however families with children who like to swim tend to prefer the slower and warmer late summer water.

Desolation and Gray Canyon, Utah

Winding through desolate canyons in central Utah, the Green river flows towards the mighty Colorado river. Towering grey stone cliffs, beautiful desert scenery, and great rapids have made this run famous. This 84-mile trip runs from from Sand Wash to Swaseys Rapid (just outside Green River Utah) and boasts outstanding scenery, interesting geologic formations, evidence of prehistoric and historic human activity, and great whitewater opportunities.

Desolation is rated Class II/III and the trip usually takes 6 days. Desolation Canyon has been recognized as a National Landmark. This designation is based on the unique features and rich history of the canyon.

Grand Canyon, Arizona

This is the grand daddy of all canyons and river trips. You will never forget your visit to the Grand Canyon. Grand Canyon river rafting combines world-class whitewater with breathtaking scenery to make one truly unforgettable river experience. The canyon is filled not only with exhilarating whitewater rapids, but with side canyons and ancient Native American ruins accessible only by river.

CLASS I-X: The Grand Canyon uses a unique river rating scale. The rapids in the Canyon are technically rated I-X (1-10) to accommodate such a wide variety of rapids and river variances. However, most people are familiar with the regular I-V river rating scale, so we often refer to both.
